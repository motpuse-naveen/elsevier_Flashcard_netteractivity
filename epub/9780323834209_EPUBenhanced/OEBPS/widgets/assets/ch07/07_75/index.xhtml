Summary of Lower Limb Arteries

Comment: The femoral artery is a direct continuation of the external iliac artery. The medial and lateral circumflex femoral arteries form an anastomosis around the hip joint, with a contribution from the obturator artery. Genicular branches of the popliteal artery (a continuation of the femoral artery posterior to the knee) form a rich anastomosis around the knee. The popliteal artery gives rise to the anterior and posterior tibial arteries, and the posterior tibial artery gives rise to the small fibular artery. The posterior tibial artery then continues into the sole and divides into medial and lateral plantar arteries.

Clinical: The major pulse points of the lower limb include:

  • Femoral pulse, just inferior to the inguinal ligament

  • Popliteal pulse, deep behind the knee (difficult to find)

  • Posterior tibial pulse, on the medial aspect of the ankle posterior to the medial malleolus

  • Dorsalis pedis pulse, felt just lateral to the flexor hallucis longus muscle tendon. Generally, this is the most distal pulse from the heart that can be easily palpated. The pulse is more obvious if the foot is slightly dorsiflexed. A diminished or absent pulse suggests vascular insufficiency and suggests peripheral vascular disease (PVD).
