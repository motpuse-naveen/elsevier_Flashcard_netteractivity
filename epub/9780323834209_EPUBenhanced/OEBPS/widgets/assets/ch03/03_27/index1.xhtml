Valves and Fibrous Skeleton of Heart

Comment: The 1st heart sound (“lub”) is caused by closure of the tricuspid and mitral valves at the onset of ventricular systole. The 2nd heart sound (“dub”) is caused by closure of the aortic and pulmonary valves at the onset of ventricular diastole.

The pulmonary valve is a semilunar valve with three leaflets. Usinga stethoscope, this valve is best heard over the left second intercostal space, just lateral to the sternum. The aortic valve also is a semilunar valve with three leaflets. Closure of this valve is best heard over the right second intercostal space just lateral to the sternum.

The mitral (bicuspid, or left atrioventricular) valve has two leaflets. Its closure at the onset of ventricular systole is best heard in the left fifth intercostal space, at the midclavicular line. The tricuspid (right atrioventricular) valve has three leaflets. Its sound is best heard over the lower portion of the body of the sternum.

The wall of the heart is composed principally of cardiac muscle and a fibrous skeleton that provides attachment points for some of the cardiac muscle fibers and each heart valve.

Clinical: The heart valve leaflets are avascular and receive their nutrients and oxygen by diffusion from the blood flowing through a valve.
