Ligaments of Elbow

Comment: The elbow joint forms a uniaxial synovial hinge (ginglymus) joint that includes the humeroradial joint (between the capitulum of the humerus and the head of the radius) and the humeroulnar joint (between the trochlea of the humerus and the trochlear notch of the ulna). The joint also includes a proximal uniaxial radioulnar synovial (pivot) joint that participates in supination and pronation (rotation). Movements about the elbow include flexion and extension.

The joint is stabilized by the laterally placed radial collateral ligament and medially placed triangular ulnar collateral ligament. The anular ligament holds the head of the radius in place.

Blood is supplied to the elbow by branches of the brachial artery and recurrent collateral branches of the radial and ulnar arteries.

Clinical: A strong pull on the forearm, especially in children, can pull the head of the radius out of the anular ligament, resulting in a dislocation of the proximal radioulnar joint. Elbow dislocations occur third in frequency after shoulder and finger dislocations. Dislocations can also injure the ulnar nerve (most common) or median nerve.
