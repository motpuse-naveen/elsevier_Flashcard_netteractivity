Larynx

X

Comment: The larynx (voice box) is a musculoligamentous and cartilaginous structure that lies at the C3-C6 vertebral level. It functions as both a sphincter to close off the airway and as a “reed instrument” to produce sound. The intrinsic skeletal muscles of the larynx attach to the laryngeal cartilages and act largely to adjust the tension on the vocal folds to open and close the rima glottidis (the space between the vocal folds). This action is important during swallowing to prevent aspiration into the trachea and during phonation. These muscles are all innervated by the recurrent laryngeal branch (inferior laryngeal nerve) of CN X.

Clinical: Excessive use of the vocal folds (singing or speaking) can cause the mucosal lining to become inflamed and lead to a hoarse voice. Likewise, a lesion to the vocal folds, such as cancer may also present initially as a hoarse voice.
